    Social Science & Medicine

    Volume 46, Issue 9, 1 May 1998, Pages 1227–1233

    Firearm suicides and homicides in the United States: regional variations and patterns of gun ownership1

    • Mark S. Kaplana, ,
    • Olga Gelingb
    • a School of Community Health, College of Urban and Public Affairs, Portland State University, P.O. Box 751, Portland, OR, 97207-0751, USA
    • b Department of Sociology, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, IL, USA
    Available online 7 July 1998

    Abstract

    Among industrialized countries, the United States has the highest rates of firearm suicide and homicide, as well as the highest rate of gun ownership. The present study compares the differential impact of gun availability on firearm suicides and homicides in the U.S. Using data from the NCHS Mortality Detail Files (1989–1991), the 1990 U.S. census population estimates, and the General Social Surveys (1989–1991) for nine geographic divisions, we computed rates of firearm and non-firearm suicides and homicides as well as rates of gun ownership for four gender-race groups. We tested the strength of the associations between gun availability and firearm suicide and homicide rates by computing the Spearman correlation coefficients. To help elucidate the role of method substitution, we conducted similar analyses on non-firearm suicide and homicide. The results show that gun ownership has a stronger impact on firearm suicides than homicides. These findings held up after stratifying by gender and race. The study suggests that reducing the aggregate level of gun availability may decrease the risk of firearm-related deaths.

    Keywords

    • firearms;
    • suicide;
    • homicide;
    • gun ownership

    1. Introduction

    The United States leads industrialized countries with the highest numbers and rates of firearm deaths (American College of Physicians, 1995; Rosenberg, 1995; Seltzer, 1994). Guns are the predominant method of homicide and suicide for both sexes in the U.S. (Seltzer, 1994). Firearm deaths are among today's top ten leading causes of death (Krieger et al., 1995) and are likely to become the primary cause of injury-related mortality by 2003 (Adler et al., 1994). Of the approximately 34 000 firearm deaths every year (Teret and Wintemute, 1993), over half are attributed to suicides. Yet, while the increasing rate of firearm homicides has given rise to much concerned comment in the lay press, the increase in firearm suicides has not generated the same media or policy attention (The Education Fund to End Handgun Violence, 1995). Although a substantial body of research indicates that the epidemic of firearm violence in the U.S. is attributed to increased availability of guns (Kellermann et al., 1993; Loftin et al., 1991; Max and Rice, 1993), very little is known about the differential effects of gun availability on firearm-related suicides versus homicides.

    1.1. Firearm availability and suicide

    Some international studies have found an association between gun availability and suicide. Killias (1990) found a significant correlation between firearm ownership and suicide in fourteen developed countries (United States, Canada, Australia, France, West Germany, Switzerland, Belgium, the Netherlands, England and Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland, Norway, Finland, Spain) participating in the International Crime Survey. He also found that countries other than the United States have low rates of firearm suicide. Killias attributed this phenomenon to the low prevalence of firearms in those countries. In Australia, Burnley (1995) found that more ready access to guns was a factor in the elevated suicide mortality among young and older males in the inland regions of New South Wales. Dudley et al. (1992) attributed higher overall and firearm suicide rates in Queensland and Tasmania from 1961 to 1985 to the lack of legislative restrictions and the higher percentage of households owning guns.

    Researchers in the United States have also established a link between gun availability and suicide. In a study comparing the nine major geographic divisions of the U.S. (New England, Middle Atlantic, East North Central, West North Central, South Atlantic, East South Central, West South Central, Mountain, and Pacific), Markush and Bartolucci (1984) found a 3.0 per 100 000 population increase in the suicide rate per 10% increase in the household prevalence of firearms. In addition, Kellermann et al. (1992) found that ready availability of firearms in the home was associated with an increased risk of suicide. After controlling for potential confounders (i.e., education, drug and alcohol use, psychotropic medication, arrests, and living alone), the presence of one or more guns was associated with a 4.8 times increased risk of suicide. Similarly, Clarke and Jones (1989) found a strong relationship between handgun ownership and the rate of firearm suicides. Their data showed that the rate of gun suicide per 100 000 population increased by more than 50% from 4.7 in 1959 to 7.2 in 1984, while there was a small decline in the rate of non-gun suicides. As noted by the authors, the increase in the overall suicide rate was accounted for entirely by the increase in gun suicides. They concluded that handgun ownership was of considerable importance in explaining the upward shift in gun suicides. Marzuk et al. (1992) found that the differential rates of suicide between five counties of New York City were in large part due to differences in accessibility to lethal methods of injury, particularly firearms. More recently, preliminary results of a study by the Harvard Injury Control Center also indicated that gun ownership levels are significantly associated with suicide (Henigan et al., 1995).

    1.2. Firearm availability and homicide

    Research conducted in several countries has suggested a connection between the prevalence of firearms and the homicide rate. In an analysis of the United States and Britain (England and Wales), Clarke and Mayhew (1988) found that the non-gun homicide rate in the U.S. was only 3.7 times greater than in Britain, whereas the rate for handgun homicides was 175 times greater. These authors concluded that the substantial differences in availability of firearms in the two countries accounted for differences in the homicide rate. Similar conclusions were reached in a comparison of homicide rates between Seattle, Washington with Vancouver, British Columbia during 1980 through 1986. In a landmark study, Sloan et al. (1988) found that despite the demographic similarities of the two cities, the rate of homicides involving firearms was 4.8 times greater in Seattle. These authors concluded that the excess risk of homicide in the U.S. was associated with a wider proliferation of firearms. In another study to determine whether firearm use was an increasing component of the general pattern of homicide in Cape Town, South Africa, between 1986 and 1991, MacDonald and Lerer (1994) reached a similar conclusion that the rise in the proportion of the homicides that involved firearms was related to the increased legal and illegal availability of firearms.

    In the United States, the relationship between firearm possession and homicide was examined by comparing different regions and cities (Newton and Zimring, 1969; Zimring and Hawkins, 1992). Newton and Zimring (1969) demonstrated that the frequency of reported gun ownership was related to the percentage of homicides and aggravated assaults committed in four geographic regions (South, West, Midwest, and Northeast) and eight cities (Atlanta, Houston, St. Louis, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Pittsburgh, New York, and Boston). Results of this study revealed that the cities with high rates of gun use in crime were in the South and West, the areas with the highest gun ownership rates. Cities located in areas with relatively low rates of gun ownership, such as New York and Boston, have the lowest rates of gun use in crime. Similar conclusions were reached in recent case-control studies conducted in the U.S. The investigators found that the presence of a gun in the home was independently associated with a 2–3 times higher risk of death by homicide (Cummings et al., 1997; Kellermann et al., 1993).

    While most previous research has focused on the effects of gun availability on suicide or homicide, to our knowledge, no study has explicitly compared the differential effects of gun ownership on suicide versus homicide in the U.S. at the aggregate level. The present study hypothesizes that the effects of gun availability on suicide differ from the corresponding effects on homicide. Due to the differential patterns of suicide and homicide mortality, we stratify the analyses by four gender and race groups (white males, white females, black males, and black females). Research shows that males have higher suicide rates than females. Whites have higher suicide rates than Blacks for both males and females ( Holinger, 1987). For homicide, rates for males are higher than those for females; and Blacks have higher rates than Whites (Holinger, 1987).

    To address the hypothesis we examine the extent to which regional variations in gun ownership are associated with regional variations in the rates of firearm suicide versus firearm homicide. We present the actual numbers and rates of firearm and non-firearm suicides and homicides by the nine geographic regions. We also examine the proportions of suicides and homicides involving firearms.

    2. Data and methods

    The data for the present analyses derived from three sources. Mortality data came from the 1989–1991 Mortality Detail Files compiled by the Division of Vital Statistics of the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) from the death certificate files, including information on the cause of death, sex, race, and the state where the death occurred. Using data for the three years helped smooth temporary fluctuations from year to year and allowed adequate numbers of cases in all cells. The International Classification of Diseases, 9th Revision (ICD-9) was used to extract all deaths identified as suicide (codes E950-E959) and homicide (codes E960-E965). The analyses excluded “undetermined” deaths that were due to injuries that could not be classified as accidental, suicidal, or homicidal (codes E980-E989). Estimates of the populations at risk derived from the 1990 U.S. census (U.S. Bureau of the Census, 1996). Estimates of gun ownership were based on data from the National Opinion Research Center's General Social Surveys (GSS) for 1989 through 1991 (Davis and Smith, 1989–1991). Each year, respondents were asked, “Do you happen to have in your home (or garage) any guns or revolvers”?
