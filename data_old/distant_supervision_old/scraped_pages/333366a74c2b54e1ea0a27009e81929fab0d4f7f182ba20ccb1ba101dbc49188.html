The Open 2012: Sergio Garcia set to be guided by spirit of Seve Ballesteros

IT IS more than a year since Seve Ballesteros passed away and time, the great healer, has eased the pain of his loss.

By Neil Squires
PUBLISHED: 00:00, Thu, Jul 19, 2012

Sergio Garcia tuning up yesterday []

Left behind are memories, so vivid in a few special places, that it is almost as if the great Spanish gunslinger is still with us. And nowhere does the spirit of Seve resonate more strongly than at Lytham.

Over the next four days a search will be conducted for a worthy successor to the genius who won two Open Championships on the unassuming Lancashire links.

It is a place where the cream has traditionally risen to the top. From the 1926 champion Bobby Jones, who had to pay to get in for his final round after forgetting his player’s pass, through to Bobby Locke, Peter Thomson and Gary Player, Lytham is A-list territory. Ballesteros remains the only man to have won two Opens here. Doing so from a temporary car park a domestic flight wide of the 16th fairway, as Ballesteros did in 1979 on the way to his first Major, is not recommended this week.

The rampant rough from this cursed summer that even a strongman such as Tiger Woods rates as unplayable will not allow for the theatrics of Ballesteros’s final round.

I can’t imagine how it would have been playing a tournament with him

Sergio Garcia

The more traditional route he followed in 1988 when he won his last Major would be a better recommendation for those who attempt to follow in his footsteps this week.

“This is extremely special – 1996 was my first Open, here at Lytham, and I got to play with Seve in practice rounds,” recalled Sergio Garcia, who will go out with Woods and Justin Rose in the star grouping today. 

“He spoke to me about some of the shots he hit when he won, but one of the things I remember most was the aura Seve had. Walking with him let you feel the energy there was around him. The people threw him their energy.

“I can’t imagine how it would have been playing a tournament with him, when it was like that during practice days.

“With everything that Seve has done here, and the way he did it, it is very important to me. I just hope I can give myself my best performance so that I am at least out there having a chance.

“Apart from the Ryder Cup, the Open is my favourite tournament. I love everything about it – not only the tournament itself, but the courses, the history behind it. I love the way the crowds embrace me.

“If you tell me I can only win one Major in my life, the Open is the one I would choose. No doubt about it.”

For Garcia, as for other Major-less men such as Luke Donald and Lee Westwood, the unlikely triumph of Darren Clarke in last year’s Open proves the elusive dream is still worth chasing.

The Spaniard, who has finished in the top five eight times at Majors, is over his moment of defeatism after this year’s Masters, when he confided he did not think he was good enough to win one.

“I have had my share of chances and when it doesn’t happen it hurts a little. But the thing is I am only 32, which in golf terms is good and hopefully if I stay healthy I have many more Majors to come and many more chances to win,” he said.

“It’s just a matter of putting myself in the right position and hope things turn the right way, as they did for Darren last year.

“I love him, but we all thought, ‘Is he ever going to win one? It’s going to be tough’. Nobody was expecting him to lead and to win but it happened. That’s the beauty of it, I guess. He did it when he was 42 so I still have a lot of years.

“Sport has a way of sometimes giving you things really quickly and sometimes making you sweat for it.

“There are guys who have chances of winning Majors and do it right away.

“For other guys, it seems like life is testing them a little bit.” The test will be unrelenting over the coming days. Lytham is an oddity, starting as it does with a par three, but its punishing finishing run of six par fours usually into the wind will sap the life force out of many.

It is a venue which will demand a mix of Garcia and Westwood’s ball-striking and the survival instincts of proven scrappers such as Padraig Harrington and Graeme McDowell.

“It is a positional course. Even the bombers can’t take all the trouble out of it. You have to thread the ball and take the trouble on,” said McDowell, the runner-up at last month’s US Open.

“If you keep the ball on the fairways you can play this golf course – if you miss the fairways you can’t.”

The par score has shrunk from 71 to 70 since the last time the event was staged here.

The 492-yard sixth – the easiest hole 11 years ago – will be one of the hardest this year having been trimmed from a par five to par four.

One thing is for sure, Lytham will be no seaside stroll. Whoever inherits the mantle of Ballesteros on Sunday evening, he will have earned it.‘I remember most of all the energy around him’
