10A-DEL RIO (TEXAS) NEWS-HERAID, Sundoy, August 15 SECOND TIME IT STICKS Opportunity and J.C. Wiley Meet in Del Rio By DICK ALWAN Kuidoso Downs Publicity Director RUIDOSO DOWNS, N.M. A few years ago, Opportunity came knocking at J.C. Wiley's door, but the young jockey was too discouraged and disgusted to know the difference. So Opportunity passed him by — the first time, at least. The second time around, Wiley was ready. He grabbed Opportunity with both list« and wouldn't let go. In just a few short months — since the start of the current Ruidoso Downs meeting — Wiley vaulted from a bush- track rider to one of the year's best quarter horse jockeys. Only 23, he's been riding on the small-time Texas circuit for the past eight years, and figures he's the No. I rider out there in the sticks. Last. December, his career got a sharp boost when he won the $110,000 Louisiana Futurity at Delta Downs on a 41-1 longshol. But generally, he says, it's been a long climb, and a pretty dismal one. Most of the time, he felt he wasn't getting anywhere. "There are a lot of good riders out there," Wiley says in a low-key and monotone voice. "They have a lot of ability, and a lot of potential. But they don't get to know the right people, they don't get any breaks ..." That was the gloomy mood Wiley was up against when he came to Ruidoso in May on a wing and prayer. The only thing he had for sure was some work as an exercise rider — plus the slim comfort of being the second-line rider for the powerful stable of Trainer Bubba Werner. "That was reaching for the moon," Wiley says. "It's pretty remote for one stable to support two jockeys. What I was hoping was that Bubba might qualify two horses for a big futurity, and I'd get to ride one of them." That, at least, was the way the situation looked in mid- May. Now look at Wiley; he's in the top 10 in jockey standings, he rides some of the best stock around, he's been in the finals of the $338,208 Rainbow Derby, the $413,150 Kansas Futurity, and the $368,784 Rainbow Futurity. He also rode Sheswright in the $84,265 World's Championship Classic, and he won the Zia Stakes with Dancing Butler. In short, that's the kind of success you dream about while knocking heads on the bush track circuit, hoping Opportunity will come along and give you a chance to show your stuff on some top stock. Wiley's first chance came about five years ago, but he let it slip through his fingers. "I was down at Del Rio, Texas, where I was riding for Trainer James Bertrand," ne explains. "But I got where I felt I wasn't getting anywhere. I felt pretty disgusted, so I didn't show up for a few days. Bertrand got mad, of course. So he quit using me as his first-call rider, and got Tad Ober. Tad and I were living together at the time, and he was my best friend." About the time Bertrand signed up Ober, 17 (a total unknown in quarter horse racing, he also got the filly, Pass Over, She turned out to be one of the truly great mares in quarter horse racing, earning $521,000. As Pass Over streaked her way to fame, she hauled Ober with her — for the first leg of the journey, at least. The youngster was aboard in 1973 when she won the Sun Country Futurity at Sunland Park Park and the Kansas Futurity at Ruidoso, for example. "If I would have stuck with Bertrand at the time, I could have been riding Pass Over," Wiley says. "I just took it as an experience. The lesson I learned was don't give up." Wiley says he never begrudged Ober his success on Pass Over, and the two remained close pals. But that part of the story came to an ironic and tragic end. One day in '74 (Ober had since lost the assignment on Pass Over), Wiley got a call at the barn where he was working. "They told me Ober had died. They said he shot himself with a .44 magnum. At least that's what they put down in the newspaper. It always puzzled me. Tad never seemed the kind to worry about anything. When they told me about it, I couldn't believe it. It was kind of hard to swallow." Wiley got serious about riding again, and resolved to stick it out. "There are a lot of boys who get depressed even before they get started," he recalled. He reasoned: "As long as there's races going on, there's going to be breaks," But he ran into "a lot of bad breaks "last year. He explains: "After being the leading rider on the bush track circuit, it seemed I just couldn't win a race. I don't know why — my horses were just getting outrun. But this time I didn't think of quitting." The downward trend was reversed in December when he qualified eighth-best with Azure. Fair in that $110,000 Futurity in Louisiana, then won the finale by three quarters of a length. "That was a great feeling. He was always a good colt, and I was hoping he'd run third or fourth. But he just had one of his good nights. Boy, that was some Christmas present." Trainer Bertrand now comes back into the picture, and asks the rejuvenated Wiley to be his first-call rider once again. So this past spring, Wiley returns to Del Rio. As luck would have it, Bubba Werner was also at Del Rio, with Dave Morris as his first-call rider. But Morris got flattened out when a horse shied from the crowd, took a left turn and went over the rail near the finish line. This left Werner without a jockey for his stakes-winning filly, Oedra'sUh Oh. Shortly after Morris' spill, as Wiley was walking back to the jockey quarters, Werner's assistant, Johnny Bustamante, was waiting for him. "You want to ride our filly?" he asked. As it turned out, Wiley's riding had caught Werner's attention. Wiley, not one to let Opportunity pass him by for a second time, shot back: "Yes, Sir! You bet!" So the next day, Wiley rode Dedra's Uh Oh in a stakes race at Del Rio, and won in blazing time. Later, Werner suggested Wiley might like to come up to Ruidoso Downs as a gallop boy and understudy to Morris (who had recovered quickly from that spill). Things took an unexpected turn in Wiley's favor when, at , the start of the meeting, Werner and Morris didn't exactly see things eye-to-eye, and dissolved their working arrangement. "Bubba told me he was going to give me a chance for two weeks," Wiley says. During that two-week period, Wiley had about nine mounts, won with four or five — including Sheswright, who zipped over 350 yards in a fiery 17 46 seconds. Werner said approvingly: "Well, I guess you can ride as fast as anyone else From that point on, Wiley s been riding first-call for Werner. So you review all this, and it seems to go back to pure chance. If Morris hadn't gotten hurt — if Werner hadn't picked him out - Wiley wouldn't be where he is today. Quite likely, he'd still be beating his brains out on the Texas flatlands, dreaming of that one chance in a big-time Ruidoso stakes race. But if it all depends on Opportunity, you can't let yourself go sour while awaiting that knock on your door, Wiley says. The secret is working hard, and getting those good mounts. "If you put out a hundred-per cent every time, you won't have any worries. Any top trainer is trying for all he's worth. What he wants is a rider who's trying just as much..."
