Measuring Efficiency of Health Systems of the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) Region Using Stochastic Frontier Analysis

Abstract

Objective

The main purpose of this study is to measure the technical efficiency of twenty health systems in the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region to inform evidence-based health policy decisions. In addition, the effects of alternative stochastic frontier model specification on the empirical results are examined.

Methods

We conducted a stochastic frontier analysis to estimate the country-level technical efficiencies using secondary panel data for 20 MENA countries for the period of 1995–2012 from the World Bank database. We also tested the effect of alternative frontier model specification using three random-effects approaches: a time-invariant model where efficiency effects are assumed to be static with regard to time, and a time-varying efficiency model where efficiency effects have temporal variation, and one model to account for heterogeneity.

Results

The average estimated technical inefficiency of health systems in the MENA region was 6.9 % with a range of 5.7–7.9 % across the three models. Among the top performers, Lebanon, Qatar, and Morocco are ranked consistently high according to the three different inefficiency model specifications. On the opposite side, Sudan, Yemen and Djibouti ranked among the worst performers. On average, the two most technically efficient countries were Qatar and Lebanon. We found that the estimated technical efficiency scores vary substantially across alternative parametric models.

Conclusion

Based on the findings reported in this study, most MENA countries appear to be operating, on average, with a reasonably high degree of technical efficiency compared with other countries in the region. However, there is evidence to suggest that there are considerable efficiency gains yet to be made by some MENA countries. Additional empirical research is needed to inform future health policies aimed at improving both the efficiency and sustainability of the health systems in the MENA region.

Key Points for Decision Makers

The inefficiency scores in health systems in MENA region provide insight into mismanagement of available resources, so interventions to improve management in health systems could help to improve efficiency.

Health systems need to provide the trained skill-mixed teams who can deliver preventive care and effective treatment at the primary care level.

A substantial investment in preventive public health programmes and promotion of healthy lifestyles accompanied by more rigorous gate-keeping is a vital step forward.

Movement from rigid input-based payment towards a performance-based payment system is urgently needed.

1 Introduction

Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region covers an area of about 11 million square kilometres and contains about 5.7 % of the world’s population. The population in MENA is very young, where the median age is about 25 years, and the percentage of people older than 65 is about 4.7 % of the total population [1]. Life expectancy increased from 67 years in 1990 to 74 years in 2013, and infant mortality rate (IMR) per 1000 live births decreased from 42 to 21 over the same period [1]. The MENA region spends about 4.7 % of its GDP (or US$403 per person) on health care, compared to 14.3 % (US$3373) by the Americas (comprising 35 countries), and 9.3 % (US$2217) by Europe [2]. The MENA health-care markets are predominantly driven by governments with government spending accounting approximately 58 % of the total healthcare expenditures. The MENA region is fairly rich as it contains 60 % of the world’s oil reserves and 45 % of natural gas reserves. Despite the levels of wealth, health insurance coverage and financial protection is limited in the MENA region. Out-of-pocket (OOP) health payments are high often leading to financial impoverishment. Estimates for the Arab world (excluding Gulf Cooperation Council) suggest that 5.5–13 million individuals face catastrophic health expenditures each year [3]. The demographic and epidemiological changes in the MENA region have a major impact on the organization and delivery of health care. The health-care needs in the region are likely to increase significantly, led by a growing population. The young population in the MENA region presented in relatively young median age, high crude birth rate, and relatively low percentage of people aged over 65, represents a tremendous opportunity both as a market and as a labour force, however, would imply that the health-care delivery system re-organization efforts should focus more on lifestyle interventions designed for disease prevention and health promotion as opposed to elderly care. However, similar to Western countries, middle and high income MENA countries face a rapid rise in non-communicable diseases (NCDs) and injuries as a share of the total disease burden [4]. Between 1990 and 2013, there was an increase in burden caused by NCDs and a drop in most communicable, newborn, nutritional, and maternal causes [5]. The top three causes of morbidity and mortality in the MENA region include; ischaemic heart disease, lower back and neck pain, and unipolar depressive disorder [6–8]. For example, low back pain and diabetes increased by 77 and 87 %, respectively, between 1990 and 2010, whereas these causes increased by 43 and 70 % at the global level. Depression ranked fifth in this region, but ranked eleventh globally. Obesity is endemic with four MENA countries, and tobacco consumption is a major risk factor for adults aged 15–59 years, especially men, but also increasingly for young women [9]. Road traffic accidents are the leading killers of young men in the region, and depression is the leading cause of morbidity among the region’s women [9]. The prevalence of NCDs demands a system that emphasises medical management and lifestyle-oriented health services. So of particular importance will be the shift from acute to chronic illnesses and the likely growing shortage of health-care workers, especially nurses and professionals. On the other hand, the prevalence of communicable diseases in low-income MENA countries demands a system that emphasises public health measures such as improved water, sanitation, and immunisation.

It has been well established in literature that inefficiencies in health spending are large [10]. World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that about 20–40 % of resources spent on health care are wasted. The most common causes of inefficiency include inappropriate and ineffective use of medicines, medical errors, suboptimal quality of care, waste, corruption, and fraud [11]. Without these inefficiencies, many countries could achieve the same level of health outcomes with a lower level of spending [12]. These facts highlight the dire need for conducting empirical efficiency analyses of the health-care systems in the MENA region to facilitate evidence-based health policy decisions. Established economic theoretical frameworks such as frontier models and particularly the stochastic frontier models are widely used in the applied economics literature.

There is insufficient evidence of widespread inefficiency in health systems in MENA. The main objective of this study is to measure the technical efficiency of 20 health systems in the MENA region at the country level. This paper applies well-established methods, and uses panel-data analysis which allowed the enlargement of a small cross-section of 20 countries into a 360 observation sample over 17 years. We systematically examine the effects of the frontier model specification on the empirical results using series of models as different models produce different parameter estimates. Since, to the best of our knowledge, our research represents the first empirical assessment of the health system efficiency in the MENA region our findings should be of particular interest to health researchers, managers, and policy makers in the MENA countries.

1.1 Literature Review

The World Health Report of 2000 considers health systems to be crucial in the development of individuals, families, and societies worldwide with three intrinsic goals: improving health, increasing responsiveness to the legitimate demands of the population and ensuring that financial burdens are distributed fairly [13]. In 2000, WHO attempted to develop econometric methodology and estimate efficiency of health systems, and presented rankings of health systems by their estimated efficiency [13]. Evans et al. [14] are credited with the first attempt to estimate the efficiency of health systems using a non-parametric approach and data of 191 countries from 1993 to 1997 and estimated efficiency. Health output was measured by healthy life expectancy and input was measured by per capita health expenditure. The results showed that France was the most efficient country in terms of health system performance and Oman ranked number eight globally [14]. Jayasuriya and Wodon [15] estimated health efficiency using stochastic frontier analysis (SFA) on a panel sample of 79 countries over the period 1990–1998. Health outcome was proxied by life expectancy, and GDP per capita, adult literacy and health expenditure per capita were used as input variables [15]. Hernandez de Cos and Moral-Benito [16] employed a SFA using panel data for 29 OECD countries with annual observation from 1997 to 2009 [16]. The results showed that Japan was the most efficient health system [17].

Akala and El-Saharty [18] note that inefficient spending on health is likely to have a substantial negative impact on economic growth and human capital development that puts additional pressure on limited governmental budgets to meet the health-care needs of vulnerable populations. The authors add that the curative model prevalent in the MENA region is becoming more difficult to sustain and is ineffective in addressing the emerging health challenges. They call for reconfiguration of the health service delivery to integrate the provision of preventive and promotion services [18]. Developing efficient and equitable delivery mechanisms is seen as a critical aspect of their proposed system changes. Similarly, Gericke [19] highlights the importance of achieving efficiency at both macro and micro levels and analysis of incentives for efficiency and quality considerations as key health-policy priorities for successful health-care financing reforms in Egypt [19]. Despite the fact that the MENA region has achieved significant improvements in key health status indicators over the past three decades, a number of MENA countries still face challenges in achieving efficient, equitable, and sustainable health services for the population served [2, 20]. Indeed, the 2010 Middle East and North Africa Health Policy Forum identified an urgent need to develop quality and productivity standards and apply these standards to measure quality, safety, and efficiency of health care in the MENA region [21].

The output in the health-care sector is conceptually difficult to define and the production of health is a complex process whose inputs include medical care, genetic and environmental factors and behaviours [22]. Most studies of health efficiency use the production function where health outcomes are modelled as the output while health resources that influence health are treated as inputs. Then, efficiency is measured by estimating the parameters of this production function and calculating the distance between the actual level of health output and the maximum level of output that can be obtained from given inputs, which is called the production frontier. Frontier regression models seek to explain the boundary, frontier or optimal behaviour rather than average behaviour as in ordinary regression models. Frontier estimation models were first discussed by Aigner and Chu [23], who fitted a Cobb–Douglas industry production function to data on production levels and factors. More generally, such models may be called frontier regression models [23].

If a production function was estimated using ordinary least squares (OLS), the resultant estimates will be located in the middle of the data points and the production frontier will not be obtained. SFA therefore, improves upon the OLS averaging process through the decomposition of the OLS error into a statistical noise component and a technical efficiency component [24]. There is no consensus on the best method to measure technical efficiency. Two main alternative approaches have been considered in the literature for estimating efficiency indices: deterministic non-parametric frontier and parametric stochastic frontier [25]. Parametric methods focus on economic optimisation, while non-parametric techniques examine technological optimisation. The most common estimation technique under the non-parametric approach is the data envelopment analysis (DEA) initiated by Charnes et al. [26]. The major advantage of DEA is that it avoids having to measure output prices which are not available for transactions and services and fee-based outputs. However, the DEA method is non-stochastic and does not capture random noise such as strikes, and any deviation from the estimated frontier is interpreted as being due to inefficiency. With DEA it is also not possible to conduct statistical tests of the hypothesis regarding the inefficiency scores.

In parametric approaches it is possible to conduct statistical tests of the hypothesis regarding the inefficiency scores. The main models under parametric approach include SFA of Battese and Coelli [27, 28]. SFA is a parametric technique of frontier estimation that assumes a given functional form for the relationship between inputs and an output [29]. Stochastic production frontier analysis has been widely used to study technical efficiency in various settings since its introduction by Aigner et al. [30], and Meeusen and van den Broeck [31]. Criticisms of parametric approaches include the need to specify in advance the mathematical form of the production function and the distributional form of the inefficiency term. The stochastic frontier production function indicates the existence of technical inefficiency of production [28, 32]. The stochastic frontier divides the distance to the frontier into random error and inefficiency. The random error takes into account exogenous shocks. The main advantage of the approach is that it accounts for the traditional random error of regression. It presents a production function of the standard regression model but with a composite disturbance term equal to the sum of the two errors components [30, 31]. The production possibility frontier is the most commonly used method to estimate the efficiency of health systems. The production possibility frontier is considered the efficient frontier as any health system production at that level is able to achieve an efficient combination of inputs. Similarly, a health system that does not produce on the efficient frontier is considered to be technically inefficient.

The implicit assumption that the inefficiency is constant over the years and the inability of distinguishing unobserved heterogeneity from inefficiency made the traditional random and fixed stochastic frontier models a poor candidate to choose. Recent literature on panel data emphasizes the importance of accounting for time-varying unobservable individual effects, which may stem from either omitted individual characteristics or macro level shocks that affect each individual unit differently [33]. Technical inefficiency measure based on traditional fixed and random effect stochastic frontier models fail to distinguish between individual heterogeneity unrelated to inefficiency and the inefficiency itself. Some SFA modelling approaches of the panel data assume a uniform variation for all decision-making units (DMUs) such as Battese and Coelli [27], and others such as Greene [34] allow for stochastic variation without any correlation over time. The latter models include three stochastic components, respectively, for efficiency, random noise and time-invariant heterogeneity. In a critique of panel studies that estimated efficiency using the SFA, Greene [34] provided evidence to show that unobserved heterogeneity can influence efficiency estimates, especially in cross-country studies, and should be accounted for. Failure to account for this may limit the reliability of the efficiency estimates and render them biased. This motivated the objective of the current study to compare estimates from various models including those that account for unobserved cross-country heterogeneity as proposed by Greene [34].

In SFA, the error term is a composite of noise (typically assumed to be normally distributed) and inefficiency (one-sided errors). Distributional assumptions made on the inefficiency term include the truncated normal, half-normal (a special case of the truncated normal), exponential, and gamma distributions; empirical work is largely based on the first two distributions. A number of studies model efficiency as a stochastic variable, often assumed to have a one-sided distribution and independent of the explanatory variables [27, 30, 35].

The econometric tools available for efficiency analysis are varied, and the field is constantly developing with regards to new techniques and methods. Stochastic frontier models for panel data are classified into two broad categories: fixed-effects models and random-effects models, with both allowing for estimation of either time-varying efficiency or time-invariant efficiency. Another issue related to the parametric specification in SFA is the assumption that parameters are constant across all observations. In health care, regulation varies across countries, so relaxing the assumption of common parameters across observations would help to account for heterogeneity across units of analysis. To do so, stochastic frontiers can be estimated using random parameters, which treat heterogeneity as a continuous function of the parameters to be estimated.

2 Methods

2.1 Data Source

We used secondary data extracted from the World Bank database for a panel of 20 MENA countries during the years 1995–2012. The choice of the time framework has been prompted by the availability of the data. The 20 countries categorised by the World Bank as the MENA region include Algeria, Bahrain, Djibouti, Egypt, Iraq, Jordan, Kuwait, Lebanon, Libya, Morocco, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Sudan, Syria, Tunis, United Arab Emirates (UAE), Yemen, Turkey and Iran. Having selected 20 countries for 17 years, we developed a balanced panel dataset with very few missing observations. The analysis is performed using STATA statistical software package (version 12.1). Panel data are desirable in SFA, as it relaxes the strong distributional assumptions for the convoluted error term (u and v), allows for correlation between the inefficiency term and inputs, and computes the conditional firm-specific inefficiency with the Jondraw Lovell Materov Schmidt (JLMS) point estimator [32, 34]. All estimations in this research are performed with STATA using the sfpanel command developed by Belotti et al. [36].

2.2 Study Variables

The selection of variables for estimation in this study is in line with the model developed by Evans et al. [14]. The authors used a production function approach for the estimation of efficiency of health-care expenditures. This present study uses Life Expectancy at Birth Years, as health-care output (dependent variable). On the other hand, we used six health-care input variables: (1) health expenditure per capita (HE): total (public and private) current expenditure including capital investment, in real values and expressed as purchasing power parities (PPPs) per capita using health-care specific; (2) school enrolment, secondary (% gross) (school): % enrolled in secondary school representing level of education; (3) improved water source (% of population with access) (WA): access to clean water and access to sanitation facilities to represent factors affecting health other than health care; (4) doctors (per 1000 people) (doctor): practicing doctors per 1000 inhabitants. (5) Incidence of tuberculosis (per 100,000 people) (TB). (6) Out-of-pocket health expenditure (% of private expenditure on health) (OOP). In line with the SFA approach, all variables are in natural logarithms. Data for the above variables are annual data and were sourced from World Development Indicators [37].

Model specification, functional form specification, and technical efficiency measurement are explained in Electronic Supplementary Material.

3.2 Stochastic Frontier Analysis

The study used balanced cross-sectional time-series panel data of 20 countries over a period of 17 years. Panel-data analysis allowed the enlargement of a small cross-section of 20 countries into a 360 observations sample over 17 years. We estimated the production function, from which efficiency scores are obtained using Cobb–Douglas function. The maximum likelihood estimates of this stochastic frontier production function model were obtained using STATA 12. We have selected three random-effects approaches. We considered random-effects model with time-invariant technical efficiency via Battese and Coelli (BC88) [38], and two specifications of time-varying efficiency models, the time-varying decay model proposed by Battese and Coelli (BC92) [27], and the true random effects model (TRE) by Greene [34, 35]. The time-invariance assumption of inefficiency is viewed restrictive, particularly if the data contain firm-specific heterogeneity, as noted by Greene [34]. The significant variables were all assuming expected signs. The greatest elasticity observed is that of the percentage of people with access to improved water source. This indicates the intense relationship that exists between production and the percentage of people with access to improved water source, independently of the utilization of other factors. The number of doctors reveals the second major factor, confirming the importance of doctors to health production. As expected, the parameters of inputs OOP and TB have the expected sign and are statistically significant at 1 % level of significance in the three models. For the most part, the estimated parameters are much in line with earlier models with regards to OOP and TB elasticities. We also found that the time trend proved to have a significant and particularly high positive effect.

In the BC88 model, 1 % increase in TB and OOP will results in 0.009 and 0.037 % decrease in production of health systems measured by life expectancy, respectively. Also 1 % increase in health expenditures per capita and number of doctors will results in 0.005 and 0.018 % increase in production of health systems measured by life expectancy, respectively. School enrolment and access to improved water source were not significant to production.

The output elasticities in the TRE model differ somewhat from our earlier SFA models. In TRE model, a 1 % increase in TB and OOP will results in 0.008 and 0.006 % decrease in production of health systems measured by life expectancy, respectively. About 1 % increase in health expenditures per capita will results in 0.001 % decrease in production of health systems measured by life expectancy. The number of doctors, school enrolment, and access to improved water source were not significant to production.

However, we concluded from the stochastic frontier analysis that BC92 is more appropriate to present data than BC88, so we will depend on the results of BC92 model. In BC92 model, a 1 % increase in TB and OOP will results in 0.001 and 0.0056 % decrease in production of health systems measured by life expectancy, respectively. Also a 1 % increase in access to improved water source and number of doctors will results in 0.0326 and 0.019 % increase in production of health systems measured by life expectancy, respectively. The output elasticity of health expenditure and school are statistically insignificant even at the 10 % level. Hernandez de Cos and Moral-Benito [16] showed that both health system efficiency and health care expenditure positively influence life expectancy with elasticity of 0.71 and 0.06, respectively.

Among the parametric models, we found that the estimated technical efficiency scores vary substantially across the model type. In addition, we observed that the time-decaying model estimates a consistently similar inefficiency to BC88, while the TRE model produces lower inefficiency estimates. In TRE, the mean inefficiencies are estimated as notably low in comparison to the time-invariant BC88 and time decay model BC92. Given the significant estimates of firm-specific heterogeneity, this seems to indicate that the BC88 and BC92 model are indeed picking up some of this heterogeneity in the composed error term, thus leading to higher inefficiency estimates. The overall results from the TRE model seem quite encouraging as the decomposition of firm-specific heterogeneity allows us to analyse efficiency in the sectors in a more precise manner. Given the time-decay model’s results of decreasing technical inefficiency over time as shown in Fig. 1, this is hardly surprising. The average technical inefficiency for all 20 countries decreased from 8.8 % in 1995 to 7.1 % in 2012.

Another somewhat surprising finding is the similarity between the time-decaying and time-invariant model. Considering that these two models rely on quite different assumptions, we would not expect this to happen. Given the specifics of our data and the time period, a flexible model that allows for efficiency to vary throughout time is most likely to give us unbiased results. To illustrate these differences, we provide a figure of the overall mean estimated inefficiencies across all countries for our three panel models, the lower estimates of the TRE model can be clearly seen as the overall mean inefficiency and is estimated to be close to 5.7 %, which even under difficult conditions is a very low estimate.

It is likely that the time-invariant model imposes too restrictive assumptions on the technical inefficiency, which can be seen in the inefficiency estimates. Conversely, when we relax this assumption with the time-decaying model we find similar results. This may be due to the fact that in relaxing the time-invariance assumption we in effect replace it with another assumption of the common efficiency trend within countries. In comparison to these models, the flexible TRE model seems to provide us with both a good fit to the data and reasonable technical efficiency estimates. 4 Discussion and Conclusion

In this paper, we estimated the technical efficiency of 20 health systems in the MENA region at the country level using SFA. We considered three alternative measures of efficiency to enhance the validity of our findings. In particular, we employed a frontier analysis using life expectancy as output in the health production function, and we considered the well-known BC88, BC92 decay model, and TRE model. The technical efficiency estimates were compared among three models using Spearman rank correlations, and were found to be of similar magnitudes. We found significant time-varying effects in the technical efficiencies of countries under analysis, particularly in true random model where country-specific heterogeneity is taken into account. Our comparisons of the estimated parameters indicate that our estimated models are not subject to considerable misspecification. The output elasticities of our chosen inputs are found to be mostly comparable among our chosen models. However, among the three parametric models, the true random effects model that allows for heterogeneity and flexible development of technical efficiency over time seems to provide the least fit to our data, yielding quite inaccurate output elasticity estimates and identifying notable trends in the technical efficiencies.

Overall, the estimated relative technical inefficiencies in health systems seem to be rather low, but with significant differences between individual countries ranging from 0.2 to 21 %. The average technical inefficiency of 20 was about 7 %. With regards to the individual country efficiency analysis, the results showed that countries like Lebanon, Qatar, and Morocco were estimated to be, relatively, the most efficient countries, while Djibouti, Yemen, and Sudan were estimated to be, relatively, the least efficient countries. Among the 20 health system characteristics evaluated in this research, access to improved water and sanitation sources and the number of doctors were found to be the strongest determinants of health-system efficiency. Health expenditures, incidence of tuberculosis, and out-of-pocket payments also proved to be determinants of health-system efficiency, but to a lesser extent.

The MENA region comprises countries at widely different levels of social and economic development. So, a one-size-fits-all approach to improve efficiency is not feasible. For example, in the poor performing countries of the region such as Djibouti, Yemen, and Sudan, there is a need to meet the basic health needs of the most vulnerable population, and child and maternal mortality rates and malnutrition among women and children is a priority, while in high performing countries such as Qatar, (which has one of the highest road accident rates in the world, and injuries as a share of the disease burden is expected to increase), preventing injuries is a priority [40].

The MENA region is far behind the developed economies such as the UK, Germany, the USA and Japan in terms of the availability of doctors and health workforce. Given the positive effect on efficiency of increasing the number of doctors, as indicated in the results, it is important to increase their number and provide the trained, skill-mixed teams including doctors, nurses and beds. Apparently, doctors have positive influence on the productivity of other inputs, so that their net contribution is positive. This is consistent with the conventional notion that doctors direct the use of non-doctor resources in health systems.

The area of health expenditures has attracted increasing policy attention and concern. The MENA region spends about 4 % of its GDP on health care, lower than the global average of 9.2 %. However, rapidly rising health-care prices and costly developments in medical technology are putting upward pressures on health budgets. Improving efficiency while containing costs is a key policy challenge in MENA countries. The results showed that increasing health expenditures in MENA will increase life expectancy. However, higher spending will not necessarily translate into effective results, especially if spending is not directed towards the most cost-effective interventions or to the priority target groups. A variety of strategic options are available, and health systems in the region show varying capacity to adopt these options. A substantial investment in preventive public health programmes, such as improved accessibility to safe water and sanitation, is crucial. It is recommended to provide skill-mixed teams that can deliver preventive care and effective treatment at the primary-care level, which will facilitate adoption of the family practice model. Promoting healthy lifestyles to reduce the impact of behavioural factors that contribute to an increasing share of burden on disease is a vital step. More rigorous gate-keeping would reduce the number of doctor consultations and improve efficiency. It is also recommended to move from rigid input-based towards a performance-based payment system. This is of particular importance in the MENA region where fee-for-service is the principle mechanism used to reimburse health providers, so there is no strong incentive to constrain the use of health services. For example, adopting a reimbursement price list that gives more value to doctor visits, as well as decreasing the reimbursement price for hospital services, surgeries, laboratory services, and radiology procedures, is expected to encourage longer doctor-patient visits and reduce the number of tests. The saved resources can be directed towards more cost-effective interventions such as health promotion and primary preventions in turn decreasing the demands for hospital services and more monetary resources [41].

Similar to previous studies on health-systems efficiency, our study has some limitations. We stress two potential limitations of our aggregate analysis before calling for country level policy recommendations. First, because a simple empirical model was used in this study, there is a possibility of the omitted variables problem, which may bias the estimation of production inefficiency. Also the inputs and outputs used in this research allowed us to perform efficiency analyses of health systems, but we should also recognise their weaknesses. Health expenditure per capita and out-of-pocket health expenditure (% of private expenditure on health) are used as proxy to economic determinants of production of health systems. Incidence of tuberculosis (per 100,000 people) is used as proxy to environmental determinants. School enrolment, secondary (% gross) is used as proxy to social determinants of production of health systems. Number of doctors (per 100,000 people) is used as proxy to health-care determinants. The labour input we use is a good standard measure and sufficiently captures the variation of labour inputs between health systems. However, health systems may also use different technologies, so we assumed that the comparison health systems use similar levels of technology. Turning to the output measure, we used life expectancy to facilitate the SFA analysis, which may not reflect the real production of health systems. Second, a relatively small sample size and a medium short time interval of 17 years may limit the generalisability of our results. Despite our best efforts to obtain the necessary information to construct our production function model, data in panel were only available after 1995 for most countries.

In conclusion, we found that most of the MENA countries are operating, on average, with a reasonably high degree of technical efficiency compared with other countries in the region. Despite this, there is evidence to suggest that there are considerable efficiency gains yet to be made by some MENA countries. This research was an attempt to measure and compare relative technical efficiency of 20 countries to inform future health policy making and health planning. It is also an invitation to other researchers in the field to apply other quantitative techniques to provide deep insight into how health systems manage their human and capital resources on a country level. Only this kind of understanding could help us to be sure that we are moving forward in our journey to enhance the efficiency and sustainability of the health systems.
