Two cheers for Theresa May putting grammar schools back on the agenda - the real work starts now

  • Joe Spence
10 September 2016 • 11:11am

Will 2016 see the death of the pundit? It probably should, but we remain embarrassingly inclined to listen to experts and to like the idea of wise seers who can help us foretell our futures, immediate and more distant.

But 2016 may well go down as the Year of the Unexpected. It all began in the early hours of Friday 24 June when, between midnight and 4am, the leaders of the Leave campaign were transformed from self-declared losers into people who would have to bear the responsibility for Brexit. And it’s gone on from there.

Many pundits declared that there could never be a resurgence of grammar schools in the United Kingdom and that it would be political suicide for a major political figure to launch a campaign to restore them. This may yet prove to be true, but it hasn’t prevented the new Prime Minister pinning her colours to the grammar school mast.

At a glance | Theresa May's school revolution

In fact, in retrospect, with a groundswell of support for grammar schools throughout the country, and with the covert expansion of numbers in existing grammar schools over recent years, sooner or later, a political leader was going to dare to draw up the sort of plan Theresa May has now announced.

The establishment of new grammar schools will put the independent sector on its mettle, wherever they are to be built. It won’t just be a question of competition for the brightest pupils. Independent schools may find teacher recruitment and retention a tougher prospect in the new order.

There was an exodus of academically-minded teachers from the former grammar schools when they were comprehensivised in the 1970s and there are good teachers in the independent sector who have always felt uncomfortable in private education who may now choose to forge careers in the new grammar schools.

The establishment of new grammar schools will put the independent sector on its mettle, wherever they are to be built

The benign scenario reads that there will now be good schools for all sorts of pupils and to suit the tastes, and even the political inclinations, of all sorts of parents.

There will be faith schools and secular schools, independent schools and academies and free schools and (hopefully) more city technology colleges to deliver first class vocational education to those who know by the age of 14 that they are not naturally of an academic bent (although I hope many of them will prove to be life-long learners and autodidacts who will discover a passion for something intellectual later in life).

What happens if you add grammar schools to the mix?

The new grammar schools will need to offer an holistic education, which independent schools already offer, as a point of difference from the ever-improving state sector.

I hope parents will demand that the new grammars also do more than teach to the test; that they will realise the value of ‘free learning’, learning undertaken free of the constraints of a rote-learnable curriculum or of an examined syllabus.

Independent education has thrived on what it has offered beyond its classrooms; in its provision of a plethora of co-curricular activities through which pupils learn those skills and aptitudes the business world is searching for in its young workforce, and which, more simply, enhance adult life: the ability to work in a team, the ability to lead and be led, the ability to communicate clearly (verbally and on paper) and the ability to think outside the box.

As a product of a grammar school education myself, what I’d hope is that, in their new incarnation, grammar schools will work hard to value all their pupils equally rather than giving undue attention to the very brightest; they must not be allowed to become exam factories.

I’ve seen too many young people leave grammar schools thinking they are stupid because they were not high-flyers in the company of the academically ablest. 

I’ve seen too many young people leave grammar schools thinking they are stupid because they were not high-flyers in the company of the academically ablest

I imagine Theresa May was in the top sets of the grammar school she believes made her the woman she is today; I wonder what the fate of those in lower sets has been and how they reflect on their schooldays?

If grammar schools can join independent schools in being the engines of social mobility, then that’s something to look forward to, but there is a need for research to establish whether the emotional belief in their social benefit can be proven.

If the creation of new grammar schools counters the postcode lottery of state education, then this too can only be a good thing.

Two cheers for Theresa May and the putting of grammar schools back on the educational and political agenda. But the real work starts now.

Britain needs to ensure access to a good education for all its young people and the return of the sort of social mobility that characterised the “white hot” 1960s.  Only then will we have leaders of the calibre we will need to navigate what it means to be proudly British in a post-Brexit Europe and to play their part in promoting the global knowledge economy in which the poorly educated (individuals and nations) will be left further and further behind.

Dr Joe Spence, master of Dulwich College
