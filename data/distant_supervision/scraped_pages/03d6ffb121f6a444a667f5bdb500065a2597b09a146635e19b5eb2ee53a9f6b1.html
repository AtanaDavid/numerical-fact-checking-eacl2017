    Financial intermediary leverage spillovers

    • Apostolos Serletisa, , , ,
    • Khandokar Istiakb
    • a Department of Economics, University of Calgary, Calgary, Alberta T2N 1N4, Canada
    • b Department of Economics and Finance, University of South Alabama, Mobile, AL 36688, United States
    Received 21 January 2016, Revised 26 February 2016, Accepted 3 March 2016, Available online 5 March 2016

    Abstract

    Using quarterly data for the United States (over the period from 1983 to 2014) and state-of-the-art financial econometrics, we explore for spillovers and interactions among the leverage levels of broker-dealers, commercial banks, and shadow banks and their volatilities. The key contribution to the literature is the estimation of a trivariate VARMA, GARCH-in-Mean, BEKK model that allows for the interdependence among the three leverage series and their volatilities. We find that broker-dealers leverage is procyclical, that there are significant spillover ARCH and GARCH effects across financial intermediaries, and that the leverage of broker-dealers is a good predictor of future economic activity.

    1. Introduction

    A consequence of financial innovation and deregulation in the financial services industry over the past 20 years was the development of a number of market-based financial intermediaries, such as finance companies, security broker-dealers, and asset-backed securities issuers. These financial institutions have become a very important component of the intermediary sector and evolved into the shadow banking system. They have been at the center of the global financial crisis and there is almost universal agreement that the global financial crisis originated in this unregulated shadow banking system. In fact, a number of recent theories give financial intermediaries a central role in economics and finance — see Geanakoplos (2010) for more details. It has been argued, for example, that financial intermediary leverage can have important effects on the level of economic activity. When leverage is high, economic agents can buy many assets with very little money down, and asset prices increase. When leverage is low, they must have all (or nearly all) of the money in hand to purchase the same assets, and asset prices decline.

    The leverage of financial intermediaries has also attracted a great deal of attention in the recent empirical literature. For example, Adrian and Shin (2010) investigate the relationship between leverage of different financial intermediaries and total assets using quarterly aggregate flow of funds data for the United States. They find that commercial banks maintain a fixed leverage ratio, irrespective of economic conditions, and that there is a positive relationship between changes in leverage and changes in total assets in the case of security broker-dealers, suggesting that broker-dealers leverage is procyclical. Istiak and Serletis (2015) integrate broker-dealer leverage shocks into an empirical macroeconomic model to bring new insights about the interaction between the real economy and financial intermediaries. Finally, Istiak and Serletis (2016) investigate the effects of leverage on the level of economic activity, using recent tests based on impulse response functions. They find that the leverage of broker-dealers is indeed procyclical and that commercial banks maintain a fixed leverage ratio and that their activities are less volatile than those of broker-dealers and shadow banks.

    However, there are no studies that empirically investigate leverage spillover and interaction effects across different sectors of the U.S. financial system, although the balance sheets of commercial banks, broker-dealers, and shadow banks are highly interconnected. As Gai and Kapadia (2010, p. 2401) put it, “in modern financial systems, an intricate web of claims and obligations links the balance sheets of a wide variety of intermediaries, such as banks and hedge funds, into a network structure. The advent of sophisticated financial products, such as credit default swaps and collateralized debt obligations, has heightened the complexity of these balance sheet connections still further. Given the procyclical and interconnected nature of the balance sheets of financial institutions, an increase in leverage in one institution amplifies the business cycle and ultimately leads to contagion and amplification effects in other institutions — see also Gai et al. (2011) and Glasserman and Young (2015).

    Our contribution in this paper is to explore for spillovers and interactions among the leverage series (and their volatilities) of the banking, shadow banking, and broker-dealer sectors of the U.S. economy. Our paper is characterized by the unique research contribution of examining the interdependence among the three leverage series and their volatilities to explore the complexity and concentration of financial linkages, which is unexplored in the existing literature. In order to perform the analysis we use a trivariate VARMA, GARCH-in-Mean, BEKK model. We find that broker-dealers leverage is procyclical, that there are significant spillover ARCH and GARCH effects across financial intermediaries, and that the leverage of broker-dealers is a good predictor of future economic activity.

    The outline of the paper is as follows. In Section 2 we investigate the time series properties of the leverage levels of broker-dealers, commercial banks, and shadow banks, using contemporaneous correlations, unit root and stationarity tests, and Hodrick–Prescott cyclical correlations. In Section 3 we estimate a trivariate volatility model that explores the interdependence of broker-dealers, commercial banks, and shadow banks leverage and volatility. The final section briefly concludes the paper.

    2. Data and basic facts

    We use quarterly leverage data for broker-dealers, commercial banks, and shadow banks from 1983:2 to 2014:4. By shadow banks we mean finance companies, funding corporations, and asset-backed securities issuers. All the leverage series are obtained from the Federal Reserve Economic Database (FRED) maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis. We follow Adrian et al. (2014) and Istiak and Serletis, 2015 and Istiak and Serletis, 2016 and define leverage as

    Total   financial   assetsTotal   financial   assets−Total   financial   liabilities.
    In doing so, and in order to avoid negative or extremely high leverage in some quarters, we subtract unidentified miscellaneous liabilities from total liabilities to measure the total financial liabilities of commercial banks and finance companies.

    Fig. 1 plots the size (in terms of total financial assets) of broker-dealers, commercial banks, and shadow banks. We see that market-based intermediaries, such as security broker dealers and shadow banks (consisting of asset-backed securities issuers, finance companies, and funding corporations) have become important components of the financial intermediary sector in the United States. In the 1980s, market-based intermediaries were a small faction of the financial intermediary sector and commercial banks were the dominant financial intermediaries with more than $2 trillion of financial assets. However, market-based intermediaries caught up with the commercial banks, mainly because of changes in housing finance in the United States. A large fraction of home mortgages in the U.S. housing market is currently financed by market-based institutions.
